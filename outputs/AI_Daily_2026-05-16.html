NASA's Psyche uses Mars flyby to gain speed for its 2029 metal-asteroid rendezvous

The spacecraft skimmed past Mars on May 15 and picked up roughly 2 kilometers per second of heliocentric velocity without burning propellant.

12,333 mphflyby speed
2 km/svelocity gain
Read full brief
  • Psyche flew past Mars on May 15 at about 12,333 mph, passing closer to the planet than either Phobos or Deimos.
  • The gravity assist added roughly 2 kilometers per second of velocity relative to the sun without requiring propellant.
  • Its target, asteroid 16 Psyche, is about 173 miles wide and may be the exposed metallic core of a shattered protoplanet.

The Mars gravity assist was one of the most important checkpoints in Psyche's journey because the mission relies on solar-electric propulsion, which is efficient but low-thrust. The maneuver redirected the spacecraft toward the asteroid belt on schedule for arrival in July 2029.

If 16 Psyche is truly exposed core material, the mission could give scientists a rare direct look at the kind of metal-rich interior normally hidden deep inside rocky worlds such as Earth.

Implications: A successful gravity-assist keeps one of NASA's most unusual planetary missions on track and advances the study of how rocky planets formed and differentiated.

Rubin found interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS before discovery, while two Jupiter probes captured a first

New reporting shows Rubin imaged 3I/ATLAS 10 days early and that JUICE and Europa Clipper made the first joint observations of an interstellar comet.

9early Rubin images
140,000 mphcomet velocity
Read full brief
  • The Vera C. Rubin Observatory imaged 3I/ATLAS nine times between June 21 and July 2, 2025, about 10 days before the comet's official discovery on July 1, 2025.
  • ESA's JUICE and NASA's Europa Clipper observed the comet's dayside and nightside simultaneously during perihelion, creating the first coordinated multi-spacecraft observation of an interstellar comet.
  • The observations found elevated carbon abundance, reinforcing earlier JWST evidence that 3I/ATLAS formed in a different solar system.

The Rubin pre-discovery images are an early proof point for the observatory's role in finding future interstellar visitors. The research suggests Rubin could become the leading detector of such objects once its full data pipeline is running.

JUICE and Europa Clipper also produced an unusually rich geometry for studying an extrasolar body by observing opposite hemispheres at once, adding rare compositional evidence about matter formed around another star.

Implications: This is a major sign that next-generation survey telescopes and multi-mission coordination are transforming how quickly scientists can detect and characterize objects from beyond the solar system.
